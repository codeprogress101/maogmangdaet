History of Daet

Daet is the capital town of Camarines Norte and one of the oldest settlements in the Bicol Region. It holds an important place in Philippine history and has grown into a center of culture, trade, and local governance.

Early Beginnings

The name Daet is believed to come from a Bikol word meaning “to make close” or “to bind,” reflecting the close-knit communities that originally lived in the area. Long before Spanish colonization, early settlers were already thriving through farming, fishing, and local trade.

Spanish Era

During the Spanish period, Daet became a mission town where Christianity was introduced. It officially became a town in the 1600s and later developed as a center of religion and governance. Many early churches and buildings from this era influenced the town’s culture and design.

American Period and World War II

Under American rule, Daet saw improvements in education and infrastructure. Schools, roads, and public services were developed. During World War II, the town suffered under Japanese occupation, but brave local fighters resisted and helped liberate the area.

Post-War to Present

After the war, Daet grew rapidly. It became the hub of government, education, business, and tourism in the province. One of its proudest landmarks is the first monument ever built in honor of Dr. José Rizal, erected in 1898.

Today, Daet is a progressive town known for its rich history, friendly people, and beautiful destinations like Bagasbas Beach and the Pinyasan Festival, which celebrates the town’s famous sweet pineapples.

A Town of Pride and Progress

From its roots as a small riverside settlement to becoming the heart of Camarines Norte, Daet continues to honor its past while moving toward a brighter future.
